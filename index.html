About

Motivated by a simple question:
Who benefits from progress?

I am a data scientist who has spent my career at the intersection of AI and labor economics, translating rigorous economic research into systems that governments and companies can actually act on. Right now, at Workhelix, I am operationalizing foundational research on how LLMs and generative AI reshape work and workers, helping Fortune 500 companies and governments measure the real workforce implications of AI adoption. One of the most significant projects I have worked on supported the Chilean government in designing and running an AI upskilling program reaching 130,000 workers.

The work at Workhelix sits at the applied frontier of labor economics and AI. The core intellectual challenge is one I find genuinely compelling: how do you take economic frameworks about task exposure and skill complementarity and turn them into tools that actually inform decisions at scale, across industries, across countries?

That question has roots in earlier work. At MaRS Discovery District, I led studies on automation risk across Canadian occupations and regions, built a job recommender engine to help workers navigate transitions, and identified the COVID-19 vulnerabilities of precarious essential workers. That research was cited by Public Health Ontario, the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia, the Labour Market Information Council, and the University of Ottawa.

The central challenge of our era is ensuring AI's gains are broadly distributed. That question is fundamentally empirical, economic, and urgent. The organizations shaping how AI is deployed will shape who benefits from it, and I want to be part of that work.
